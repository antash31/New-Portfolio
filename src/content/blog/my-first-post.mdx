# Why I'm Building in Public

There's a version of this that sounds noble: *"I want to help people by sharing what I learn."* And that's true. But the deeper reason is selfish in the best way.

## Accountability through transparency

When you publish your process, you can't hide from it. Every half-finished project, every abandoned idea, every week where you shipped nothing — it's all on the record. That pressure is a feature, not a bug.

> The best accountability partner isn't a person. It's an audience.

## The compounding effect

Every post I write becomes a reference I can link back to. Every framework I publish gets stress-tested by strangers. Every mistake I document saves me from repeating it.

This isn't content marketing. It's **thinking infrastructure**.

## What this looks like in practice

Here's a typical week:

```
Monday    → Ship one feature, write the technical note
Tuesday   → Research + AI experiments, document results
Wednesday → Record one video or write one essay
Thursday  → Build systems / automation work
Friday    → Review metrics, plan next week, publish recap
```

The cadence matters more than any individual output. Consistency compounds.

## The invitation

If you're building something — anything — consider doing it in public. Not for the likes. Not for the leads. For the clarity it forces and the community it attracts.

The door is open. Come build with me.
